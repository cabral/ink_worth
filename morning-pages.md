# Morning Pages

Morning Pages come from Julia Cameron's *The Artist's Way*, which calls them the bedrock tool of the whole method. The practice is three pages, handwritten, first thing in the morning. No editing, no reading back, no audience. You write whatever is in your head until three pages are full, then you stop.

That is the entire rule. Everything below is detail about how to do it and what tends to happen once you do.

I put this first on the site because it's the cheapest way to prove the idea the whole project runs on: your thoughts earn a page, however ordinary they are. Putting them there is the practice.

## How to start

You need a notebook and a pen. That is the complete list.

You do not need a good notebook. A nice one can work against you, because a beautiful blank page asks you to write something worthy of it, and worthy is exactly what Morning Pages are not for. A cheap spiral notebook you'd happily fill with nonsense is closer to the right tool. You don't need a particular pen, an app, a prompt, a clear desk, or a quiet hour carved out in advance. If you're waiting for the right setup, the setup has become the thing you're doing instead of writing.

Write by hand. This part isn't optional in Cameron's version, and the reason holds up. Handwriting is slow. It runs at about the speed of honest thinking, fast enough to keep up with your head but too slow to get clever or start arranging sentences. Typing is quicker and cleaner, which sounds like an advantage and isn't. The friction of the pen is doing real work.

Three pages at standard notebook size comes to roughly 750 words. The number is deliberate. It's a bit more than you'll have easy things to say, so you write past the obvious complaints and lists into whatever is sitting under them. Stop short of three and you quit before you get there. Go well past three and it turns into a project, which is its own kind of avoidance.

Do it first thing in the morning, before the day loads, before email and the phone and the day's plan have your attention. Cameron's claim, and my experience of it, is that the early-morning brain hasn't switched its editor on yet, so things reach the page that a wider-awake version of you would have caught and tidied away.

The sequence is short. Wake up, sit down, write until the third page is full, stop. Don't fix spelling, don't reread what you wrote, and don't show it to anyone. Close the notebook and go have your day.

## What to expect in the first weeks

The first mornings feel pointless. You'll write that you don't know what to write. You'll note what you need from the shop, complain about your sleep, describe the weather. This looks like failure and isn't. The dull, complaining, list-making material is the static, and getting it onto the page is how you clear it. There's no way through it except through it.

Somewhere around the second or third week the writing tends to shift without announcing itself. You'll catch yourself talking your way out of a worry that had been circling for days, or settling a decision on the page before you consciously knew you were deciding. It rarely feels like a breakthrough in the moment. You mostly notice it afterward.

Don't read the pages back during this stretch. Cameron suggests leaving them unread for the first eight weeks, and the reason is practical: rereading invites you to judge them, judging invites you to improve them, and once you're trying to write good pages you've lost the thing that made them useful. They're for getting thoughts out, not for keeping.

Resistance arrives on schedule. Some mornings you'll be sure it's a waste of time, or you'll suddenly find urgent reasons to do something else first. That feeling is part of the practice rather than a verdict on it. Write the three pages anyway, including the part where you write about not wanting to write the three pages.

## When you skip days

You will skip days. You'll travel, get sick, sleep through the alarm, or lose the morning before you reach the notebook. Expect it.

A missed morning is not a failure and not a reason to stop. There's no streak here worth protecting, and treating it like one only builds the moment where you break the streak and decide the whole thing is finished. Miss Tuesday and you write on Wednesday. You don't do six pages to make up the gap; catch-up pages turn the practice into a debt, and it isn't one.

Missing one morning rarely matters on its own. What can matter is the story that grows out of it, the one where you're "someone who couldn't keep it up." The gap and the story are separate things, and only the story ends the practice. A week away, or a month, costs you nothing but the days you didn't write. However far you've drifted, the way back is the same as the way in.

Open the notebook tomorrow morning and fill three pages.
